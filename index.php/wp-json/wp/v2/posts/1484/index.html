From the mid-1980s into the early 1990s, the greatest athlete of all time streaked across American sports and popular culture. Stadiums struggled to contain him. Clocks failed to capture his speed. His strength was legendary. His power unmatched. Video game makers turned him into an invincible character\u2014and they were dead-on.\u00a0He climbed (and walked across) walls, splintered baseball bats over his knee, turned oncoming tacklers into ground meat. He became the first person to simultaneously star in two major professional sports, and overtook Michael Jordan as America\u2019s most recognizable pitchman. He was on our televisions, in our magazines, plastered across billboards. He was half man, half myth.<\/p>\n

Then, almost overnight, he was gone.<\/p>\n

He was Bo Jackson.<\/p>\n

Drawing on an astonishing 720 original interviews, New York Times bestselling sportswriter Jeff Pearlman captures as never before the elusive truth about Jackson, Auburn University\u2019s transcendent Heisman Trophy winner, superstar of both the NFL and Major League Baseball and ubiquitous \u201cBo Knows\u201d Nike pitchman. Did Bo really jump over a parked Volkswagen? (Yes.) Did he actually run a 4.13 40? (Yes.) During the 1991 flight that nearly killed every member of the Chicago White Sox, was he in the cockpit trying to help? (Oddly, yes. Or no. Or \u2026 maybe.)<\/p>\n

Bo Jackson isn\u2019t Jim Thorpe.<\/p>\n

He\u2019s not Deion Sanders, either.<\/p>\n

\u00a0No, Bo Jackson is Paul Bunyan.<\/p>\n

\u00a0The Last Folk Hero is the true tale of Bo Jackson that only \u201cmaster storyteller\u201d (NPR.org) Jeff Pearlman could tell.<\/p>\n
